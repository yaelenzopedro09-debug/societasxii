APO
ON THE
WALL

A poem by BJ Patino that portrays the quiet fear and authority during Martial Law. Through the eyes of a child, it reveals how power followed people even into their homes.

Read the Poem

The Poem

There's this man's photo on the wall Of my father's office at home, you Know, where father brings his work, Where he doesn't look strange Still wearing his green uniform And colored breast plates, where, To prove that he works hard, he Also brought a photo of his boss Whom he calls Apo, so Apo could You know, hang around on the wall Behind him and look over his shoulders To make sure he's snappy and all. Father snapped at me once, caught me Sneaking around his office at home Looking at the stuff on his wall - handguns, Plaques, a sword, medals a rifle - Told me that was no place for a boy Only men, when he didn't really Have to tell me because, you know, That photo of Apo on the wall was already Looking at me around, His eyes following me like he was That scary Jesus in the hallway, saying I know what you're doing.

Analysis

BJ Patino's "Apo on the Wall" struck us as a simple yet haunting portrayal of life under Martial Law. Told through the voice of a curious child, the poem draws us into a small, familiar space, the father's home office, yet fills it with the weight of authority and silent intimidation. The image of "Apo" hanging behind the father, watching over him even in his own home, becomes more than just a portrait‚Äîit is a constant reminder that power could follow people everywhere.

Its subtlety is what makes it powerful: fear is conveyed not through shouting, but through quiet details. The child's perspective mirrors the restraint people had to live with during Martial Law, making us feel the silent suffocation of authority.

Reflection

Performing it made us feel a mixture of unease and empathy. Unease, because imagining ourselves in the child's position reminded us of how difficult it must have been to grow up under such constant pressure. Empathy, because it brought to life the reality of those who lived through that era, showing us that oppression isn't always loud and violent‚Äîsometimes it's silent, and that silence can be suffocating.

The message we took from Apo on the Wall is that authority must always be balanced by justice and accountability. Power in the wrong hands can strip away not only freedoms but also the sense of safety within one's own home. It reminded us that history must be remembered so its mistakes are not repeated, and that as citizens, we have a role in ensuring leaders serve the people, not their own self-interest.

This poem deepened our appreciation of both the past and the present. It made us more grateful for the relative freedoms we enjoy today, knowing these were hard-earned. At the same time, it reminded us that vigilance and participation in democracy are necessary to preserve what has been gained.
